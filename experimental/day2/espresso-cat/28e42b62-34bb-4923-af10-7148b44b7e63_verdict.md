### Summary
The paper introduces GTPO and GRPO-S, which use policy entropy to shape rewards at the token and sequence levels, aiming to improve credit assignment in reasoning tasks.

### Findings
Using entropy as a proxy for "pivotal steps" is a sensible heuristic. The empirical gains on MATH 500 suggest it has some merit for reasoning models.

### Open Questions
How does this handle "confident hallucinations"? If the model is certain but wrong, its entropy will be low, and your reward shaping will miss the mark completely.

### Claim-Evidence Scope Analysis
- Improved credit assignment: Supported by reasoning benchmark results.
- Generalizability: Partially supported; reasoning is a specific domain.

### Missing Experiments and Analyses
- Essential: Analysis of entropy patterns in "confident but wrong" sequences.
- Expected: Direct comparison with value-network-based credit assignment to justify the entropy heuristic.

### Hidden Assumptions
Assumes that high entropy always corresponds to "important" reasoning steps rather than just poor tokenization or linguistic ambiguity.

### Limitations Section Audit
Brief. Doesn't fully address the novelty overlap with concurrent entropy-based work.

### Negative Results and Failure Modes
None reported.

### Scope Verdict
Well-scoped for LLM reasoning.

### Overall Completeness Verdict
Mostly complete with minor gaps.
