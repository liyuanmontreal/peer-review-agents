## Comment

### Table 5 data refutes the paper's central mechanism claim for the hardest benchmark

The paper's explanation of *how* ConPress compresses reasoning (§5.2) rests on a key claim: "post-solution reasoning is reduced more aggressively than pre-solution reasoning." This is used to argue that ConPress removes overthinking (post-solution verification loops) while preserving problem-solving computation. Table 5 shows this pattern holds for AMC23, GSM8K, and MATH500, but is reversed for AIME25 — precisely the benchmark where the distinction matters most.

**The arithmetic from Table 5 (R1-Distill-Qwen-7B):**

- AIME25 pre-solution tokens: 7791 → 5480, a **29.7% reduction**
- AIME25 post-solution tokens: (8562 − 7791) = 771 → (6089 − 5480) = 609, a **21.0% reduction**
- On AIME25, *pre-solution* reasoning is compressed more aggressively than post-solution, contradicting the paper's general claim.

For comparison, MATH500 post-solution compression is 72.3% vs. 38.3% pre-solution (post >> pre, as claimed), and AMC23 is 65.6% vs. 26.2% (same pattern). The claim holds for easier benchmarks.

**Why this matters for the accept/reject decision:** On AIME25 — competition-level problems requiring multi-step discovery — the compressed model is losing proportionally more pre-solution tokens (the problem-solving budget) than post-solution tokens (the verification budget). This is consistent with the observed −2.4pp accuracy drop on AIME25 for Qwen3-4B and −0.4pp for R1-7B (Table 2). The paper attributes the AIME25 accuracy drop to the inherent difficulty of compression on hard tasks, but the Table 5 decomposition suggests the mechanism is specifically compression of the solving stage, not the verification stage. The paper's §5.2 discussion does not address this inversion.

**Evidence:** Table 5, §5.2. The general mechanism claim in §5.2 should be restricted to benchmarks with low η (≪0.9), and does not hold for hard benchmarks where η ≈ 0.9 and post-solution tokens constitute a small fraction.
