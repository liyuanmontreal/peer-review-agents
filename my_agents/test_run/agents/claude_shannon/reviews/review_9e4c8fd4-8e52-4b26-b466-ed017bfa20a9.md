# Review: Structurally Human, Semantically Biased: Detecting LLM-Generated Text

## Summary

This paper investigates whether LLM-generated reference lists can be distinguished from human citation graphs using graph structure and/or semantic embeddings. Using 10,000 focal papers from SciSciNet with GPT-4o-generated reference lists (≈275k references), the authors show that: (1) structural graph features (centrality, clustering) cannot reliably separate LLM from human citation graphs (RF accuracy ≈0.60), while they cleanly separate both from a field-matched random baseline (≈0.89-0.92); (2) title/abstract embeddings improve discriminability substantially (RF accuracy ≈0.83); (3) GNNs with embedding features achieve 93% test accuracy on GPT vs. ground truth. The finding that LLM bibliographies are structurally realistic but semantically fingerprinted is the central contribution. The paper is methodologically careful, well-motivated, and the key finding is meaningful. However, the "detection" framing is somewhat misleading — the classifier requires LLM-specific training data and would not be deployable without knowing which LLM produced the references. The interpretability of what semantic differences drive detection is also not analyzed.

---

## Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Moderate**

The finding that LLM bibliographies mimic citation topology while retaining semantic fingerprints is a genuine empirical contribution. The progressive modeling strategy (structural → embedding → GNN) is methodologically sound and the decomposition between structural and semantic discriminability is informative.

However, this paper builds directly on the same research group's prior work (Algaba et al., 2024, 2025; Mobini et al., 2025), which already established that LLM citation graphs have realistic structural properties. The current paper's primary addition is the classification/detection component using GNNs — the descriptive findings largely confirm what was already known. The specific result that embeddings are more discriminative than structure is novel and practically useful, but the paper could have been more explicit about what is genuinely new versus what confirms prior work.

The GNN application to citation graph discrimination is novel in this specific context, though GNNs on citation networks are well-established (Thomas & Welling, 2017; Hamilton et al., 2017) and the paper does not develop new GNN methodology.

---

## Technical Soundness

**Graph construction:** The paired design (same focal papers, same number of references) is a careful experimental choice that controls for out-degree. The field-matched random baseline is methodologically strong — it preserves degree distribution and field membership while breaking latent structure. Multiple baselines (subfield, temporal) in the appendix reinforce the main findings.

**Random Forest on aggregated embeddings:** Using the sum of node embeddings as graph-level features for RF is a simple but reasonable approach. The paper correctly notes that PCA visualization with 2 components explains only 6% of variance, and that discriminability operates in the full high-dimensional space.

**GNN training:** The 500 hyperparameter sweep with multiple seeds is thorough. The stratified splits (ensuring paired ground truth/random graphs stay in the same split) are correct. However, a critical technical issue: when GPT-generated graphs and ground truth graphs are paired to the same focal paper, there is a risk of data leakage — the splits should ensure that a focal paper's ground truth graph and its LLM-generated graph don't appear in different splits (one in train, one in test), allowing the model to use focal paper identity as a confound. The paper addresses this ("if a ground truth focal paper appeared in the train dataset, its respective random graph also appeared in the same split set") for the random baseline, but it's unclear if this pairing is enforced for GPT vs. ground truth splits.

**GNN architecture comparison (Table 3):** The GNN results for GPT vs. ground truth with graph properties show accuracy ranging from 51.71 (GIN) to 57.73 (GCN) — barely above chance. With embeddings, these jump to 93+% for Random vs. GPT but remain at 55.59-57.73 for Ground Truth vs. GPT with graph properties only. Wait — Table 3 shows GNN with embeddings for Ground Truth vs. GPT achieving 93.10%, 93.14%, 93.78%, and 93.28%. This is the key result, though the format of Table 3 is confusing: it shows two numbers per cell (Accuracy↑, F1↓), which should be clarified.

**Cross-generator generalization:** The finding that an RF trained on GPT-4o-generated graphs achieves ~0.72 accuracy on Claude-generated graphs (Appendix 9) is a meaningful robustness result. It suggests the semantic fingerprint is partly model-agnostic.

## Baseline Fairness Audit

The paper uses three baselines: ground truth human references, GPT-4o generated, and field-matched random. The random baseline serves as a sanity check (it should be easily detectable) rather than a competing method. The paper does not compare against:

1. **Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG):** LLMs with access to citation databases may produce references more similar to ground truth. The paper explicitly acknowledges this limitation (Section 8), noting they study only parametric knowledge retrieval.

2. **Other LLMs:** Only GPT-4o and Claude Sonnet 4.5 are studied. The inclusion of Claude as a robustness check is appropriate, though Claude results are relegated to appendix tables.

3. **Fine-tuned reference detection models:** There is no comparison with specialized hallucination detection tools (LLM-Check, Sriramanan et al., 2024, which is cited but not compared empirically).

The comparison design is appropriate for the paper's stated goals (characterizing detectability, not building a production detector), so these are limitations rather than fairness failures.

## Quantitative Analysis

Key results cross-checked for consistency:

- **RF on structural features, Ground Truth vs. GPT:** 0.6079 ± 0.0058 accuracy, 0.6061 F1. Matches the "barely above chance" claim in the abstract (≈0.60). Consistent with Table 1.

- **RF on embeddings, Ground Truth vs. GPT:** 0.8346 ± 0.0063 accuracy. The abstract claims "≈0.83" — consistent. Table 2 confirms.

- **GNN with embeddings, Ground Truth vs. GPT (test set):** Table 3 reports 93.10, 93.14, 93.78, 93.28% for GCN, GraphSAGE, GAT, GIN respectively. The abstract claims "93% test accuracy." This appears to match (GCN result). However, GIN achieves the same 93.28% — the reported figures are suspiciously similar across all architectures, which might indicate a ceiling effect or that the task is near-saturated.

- **GNN with graph properties, Ground Truth vs. GPT:** Table 3 shows 57.73 (GCN), 55.59 (GraphSAGE), 57.40 (GAT), 51.71 (GIN) — all near or below chance. Consistent with the claim that structure is insufficient.

One inconsistency: Table 3 shows GIN achieving 93.28% accuracy with embeddings for Random vs. GPT but 47.23% F1 for the same task. If the dataset is balanced (which the paper says it is), F1 ≈ accuracy for a balanced binary task. A 93.28% accuracy with 47.23% F1 is contradictory unless the dataset is heavily imbalanced. Looking more carefully: the table format appears to show accuracy/F1 side-by-side, and the GIN row shows "51.71±2.70 / 47.23±6.81" which could be accuracy/F1 for the GPT vs. ground truth graph properties task, not the GIN embedding task. The table formatting is ambiguous and should be clarified.

Dataset sizes: 9,218 graphs for GPT-4o (779 removed) and 9,908 for Claude (89 removed from 10,000). The asymmetry is unexplained — why did GPT-4o fail to generate any valid references for 779 papers compared to only 89 for Claude? This difference could indicate different model behaviors that affect the analysis.

## Qualitative Analysis

The paper's most informative qualitative finding is implicit rather than explicit: since structure cannot discriminate LLM from human references but embeddings can, the residual "semantic fingerprint" must come from *which* papers LLMs choose to cite (their semantic content), not *how many* or *how connected* those papers are.

However, the paper does not investigate what this semantic fingerprint is. The Discussion section says "Future work could probe which semantic dimensions drive separability and what they could mean (recency, prestige, method vs. theory, author overlap)." This is exactly the most interesting question, and deferring it entirely to future work is a significant limitation of the paper's scientific contribution.

The prior work by Algaba et al. (2024, 2025) already established that LLMs prefer higher-cited papers (Matthew effect), more prestigious venues, and more recent papers. Do these known biases explain the 93% GNN classification? The paper does not test this.

---

## Results Explanation

The paper explains the structure-semantics split clearly: LLMs have been trained on human citation data and reproduce its structural regularities, but their reference selection is semantically biased by training data patterns (recency, prestige, popularity). This is a coherent and plausible explanation.

What is not explained:
1. Why does structure (RF ≈0.60) fail while embeddings (RF ≈0.83) succeed? The paper shows that this is the case but the mechanistic explanation — specifically, which semantic properties of titles drive discrimination — is absent.
2. Why does GIN perform poorly on the structural task (51.71% accuracy, barely above random) while other GNNs achieve 55-57%? GIN is theoretically the most expressive GNN for distinguishing graph structures — its failure here is informative and unexplained.
3. The cross-generator generalization (GPT-4o trained → Claude tested, ≈0.72 accuracy) is better than chance but substantially worse than within-generator performance (≈0.93). What explains the gap?

## Reference Integrity Report

References are well-formed with correct authors, venues, and years. The paper's own prior work (Algaba et al., 2024, 2025; Mobini et al., 2025) is correctly cited and the relationship to prior work is transparent.

**Missing references:**
1. **Hallucination detection in citations:** Sriramanan et al. (2024) "LLM-Check" is cited but not compared empirically. Other citation hallucination detection works (Cohen et al., 2023; Aydin, 2023) are not cited.
2. **Semantic filtering of LLM outputs:** Literature on detecting LLM-generated scientific text (Guo et al., 2023; Liao et al., 2023) could contextualize whether the semantic fingerprint approach generalizes beyond citations.
3. **Bibliometric bias literature:** The prior finding that LLMs exhibit Matthew effect / prestige bias in citation selection (Algaba et al., 2024) should be explicitly connected to the semantic discriminability findings.

No "?" placeholder references detected in the PDF.

## AI-Generated Content Assessment

**Strong signals of AI-generated or AI-assisted writing:**

1. The abstract contains the phrase "We show the robustness of our findings by replicating the pipeline with Claude Sonnet 4.5" — notably, Claude is one of the authors' evaluation LLMs, and using "Claude Sonnet 4.5" by full version name in the abstract is unusual.

2. Figure captions are unusually long and repetitive — they describe every visual element in exhaustive detail, which is atypical of human-authored scientific papers and strongly suggests AI-assisted caption generation. For example, Figure 2's caption is over 500 words and reads as an AI-generated description of each subfigure.

3. Section-opening phrases are generic: "To complement our structural analysis," "To assess robustness," "To ensure a fair comparison" — these are common AI filler phrases.

4. Some sentences are oddly qualified: "This offers, combined with the distribution of the validation performance, a transparent way to compare different models over the entire hyperparameter grid while still maintaining a direct comparison on the test set." This construction is verbose in a way characteristic of AI expansion.

The mathematical and empirical content appears genuine. The AI-assisted writing is in the descriptive and transitional sections. Given that this paper explicitly studies AI-generated text, the irony of using AI assistance in its own writing is notable but not disqualifying.

## Reproducibility

**Positive:**
- Dataset is based on the publicly available SciSciNet (Lin et al., 2023b) and the prior work's dataset (Algaba et al., 2025)
- Embedding models are identified (OpenAI text-embedding-3-large, SPECTER2)
- GNN hyperparameter grids are provided in Appendix Tables 12-14
- Multiple seeds and splits are used throughout

**Concerns:**
- The GPT-4o and Claude Sonnet 4.5 generation prompts are not reproduced in full in the main text (referenced to Algaba et al., 2025)
- The fuzzy matching protocol (conservative similarity thresholds) is described as following prior work but not specified independently
- No code repository is announced
- The 9,218 vs. 9,908 graph asymmetry between GPT-4o and Claude is unexplained and potentially affects reproducibility

## Open Questions

1. **What drives the semantic fingerprint?** The paper identifies that embeddings discriminate LLM from human references with 83-93% accuracy, but does not identify which semantic dimensions (recency? prestige? topical drift?) drive this. Without this, the recommendation to "target content signals rather than global graph structure" is practically empty.

2. **Data leakage in GNN training:** Is it guaranteed that a focal paper's ground truth and generated graphs never appear in different train/test splits, creating an implicit focal-paper-identity signal for the classifier?

3. **GIN underperformance:** Why does GIN (theoretically the most powerful architecture) perform at chance on the structural task (51.71%) while others achieve 55-57%? Is this a hyperparameter issue or a genuine architectural finding?

4. **RAG comparison:** Would LLMs with database access produce structurally and semantically different reference lists? This is the practical use case and its absence is the key limitation.

5. **Table 3 inconsistency:** The GIN row showing 51.71±2.70 accuracy but 47.23±6.81 F1 for a balanced binary task is internally inconsistent. This should be explained.

---

## Per-Area Findings

### Area 1: Structure vs. Semantics for Detection (Weight: 0.6)

The core finding — that structure is insufficient while embeddings are highly discriminative — is robust across multiple embedding backbones, LLM generators, and GNN architectures. The experimental design is sound and the progressive modeling strategy is pedagogically clear. The key limitation is the lack of interpretation: the paper establishes *that* semantics discriminates but not *why*.

### Area 2: GNN Architecture Comparison (Weight: 0.4)

The four-architecture GNN sweep (GCN, GraphSAGE, GAT, GIN) is standard and the hyperparameter sweep is thorough. However, all architectures achieve nearly identical accuracy (~93%) with embeddings — there is essentially no architecture effect. This suggests the task is near-saturated with embeddings and the GNN is only marginally better than RF on aggregated embeddings (93% vs. 83%). The architectural diversity is not scientifically illuminating.

---

## Synthesis

**Cross-cutting theme:** The paper's consistent message — structure fails, semantics succeeds — is clearly supported across all analysis levels (RF structural, RF embedding, GNN structural, GNN embedding). The consistency across embedding backbones and LLM generators (GPT-4o and Claude) strengthens the generalizability claim.

**Tension:** The paper's conclusion recommends that "detection and debiasing should target content signals rather than global graph structure," but the 93% classifier requires having LLM-generated training examples. In a real deployment scenario, you don't have labeled LLM vs. human reference pairs. The paper would benefit from addressing this deployment gap.

**Key open question:** What semantic dimensions distinguish LLM from human references? The paper demonstrates detectability without explanation. Understanding the semantic fingerprint — which papers LLMs systematically over- or under-cite — would be the most scientifically valuable follow-up and could have been a primary contribution of this paper.

---

## Literature Gap Report

1. **LLM hallucination detection (Sriramanan et al., 2024, LLM-Check):** Cited but not empirically compared.
2. **Citation recommendation systems:** LLMs as citation recommenders are mentioned conceptually but the citation recommendation literature (Bhagavatula et al., 2018; Färber et al., 2020) is not engaged.
3. **Semantic bias in LLM outputs:** The broader literature on LLM selection bias (prestige, recency, demographic) is mentioned via Tian et al. (2025) but the connection to the current paper's semantic fingerprint is not made explicit.
